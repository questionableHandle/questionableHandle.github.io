Main Observations

Construction Consistency

The construction of the casing stones still remaining is, in many ways, much more consistent than we might expect from only the second ever attempt at building such a large structure. These casing stones are still here 4500 years later, and the majority of courses are straight, even and well fitted.

With that said: there are also significant consistency issues with what we can see of the North Face, and to some extent the South Face. The East Face, in contrast, is much neater. The North Face has significant upwards bulging of the center of its stone courses, and multiple sections of "wobbly" masonry above this area, which is likely due to the difficulty of juggling between a desire for straight, even courses and needing to correct for the earlier bulging issues. The South Face is straight on average, but also contains several "wobbly" masonry courses, mostly centered around individual stones which are taller or shorter than they should be.

The height of the casing stones is maintained within a course around the faces of the pyramid (i.e. from north to east to south), and the statistical distribution of the stones within each of these unified courses appears to be consistent. This suggests a singular source and quarrying directive supplying each unified course.

Note how the highlighted courses all have lower average width, across all three faces. They are surrounded by courses above and below with higher average width. The images of the masonry orientation (below) give a good comparison of the "straightness" of the different faces. The North Face is obviously the outlier, but note that both the East and South faces have some issues at the very top, with gaps appearing between casing stones and angled masonry occuring. The right side of the East Face joins with the left side of the North Face, and the clockwise and anticlockwise angles illustrated below pair up between them. This shows the top North-East corner is slightly sunken, probably a result of the larger issues further down the North Face.

The right of the South Face joins with the left of the East Face. The angles between these two do not align, as the East is level while the South is angled clockwise. This suggests that at these upper levels, the southern courses are slightly higher than the corresponding eastern courses. Note also, that the top left stones of the South Face, have slid to the left of their original positions.

Bulging in the North Face

There is very visable upwards deflection (bulging) of the courses on the North Face. By examining the expected vertical position of courses based on the height of casing stones, it is clear that the corners of the courses are at the correct position, and the centre is located higher up than it should be. This construction issue occured below the current level of the casing stones, and is not present on the other faces (I also visually inspected photos of the West Face and couldn't see any).

By observing the contribution to the total deflection from each course, we can see that for the entirety of the height of casing stones still remaining, the North Face builders were trying to correct for the positive vertical deflection which must have originated further down the pyramid.

Joining, or fitting, stones

As has been noted by the youtube channel "History for Granite" in his video on the casing stones, there appear to be smaller stones whose purpose is to join sections of masonry which were built concurrently.

These smaller fitting stones appear to be used to join regions of a course built by seperate workcrews, as the distribution of these small stones is consistent with the vertical splitting the face into sections of more even size.

By attempting to plot lines which seperate the face into sections (below right), you can see a somewhat symmetrical pattern, suggesting there was some attempt by the builders to spread the workcrews evening across the avaliable space (along each course). The fact that these joining stones do not align in a neat pattern to the courses immediately adjacent above and below, suggests that the horizontal courses were constructed in full before moving on to the next, instead of constructing multiple courses simultaneously. In contrast to this, the chains of offset stones section suggests the opposite.

The edges of each course follow a distinctive alternating pattern. A wide corner stone is then followed above by a narrower corner stone, and a second (and sometimes third) stone which extends to overlap the first stone.

Angles on the vertical edges of stones

Overall, left edges (of stones) are on average slightly clockwise rotated, and right edges are slightly anti-clockwise rotated, which makes sense due to the strongly angled edges of the cornerstones. Without the cornerstones in the analysis, edge angles average out to vertical.

When considering stones on the left half of the face, vs those on the right, it can be seen that both vertical edges are slightly more likely to be angled in the same direction as the nearest edge of the face (by around 1.2¬∞). This tendancy for edges to be angled in the same direction as the pyramid edge, is also exhibited on corner stones' non-corner edge.

The above images show the clustering of clockwise angles (red) on the left half of the faces, and anticlockwise (blue) on the right half. This phenomenom is not random chance, but seems too small to be a concious effort by the builders. This leaves either: an unintentional effect of the construction process, or "external" causes such as slumping or earthquakes. If the (internal) central part of the pyramid was more solid, then the outsides of the pyramid coule have settled to a slightly lower height as more weight was piled up on the pyramid. Even if the builders accounted for this issue by re-levelling each course as they went, it could leave these angular defects.

Chains of offset stones

There are "chains" of stones, wherein each stone in the chain is offset from the previous by a set amount, and the size of the stones in the chain are either similar, or decrease slowly as you go up courses. The number of chains occuring suggest these aren't random chance, and are somehow related to the construction strategy of the casing stones.

Data and Methodology

Explanation of data and methods

I started from the sketched images of the casing stones, found in this youtube video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=43V9RTpnxbk. I did some cleaning and editing of these images before using them for my analysis.

I mainly used OpenCV to do the image processing.

Image scaling and pyramid dimensions

Internal Dimensions Pyramid Face Dimensions
Internal, vertical pyramid dimensions. A vertical slice down the center of the pyramid. External, sloping pyramid dimensions. The entirety of a single face.

The (original) height of the pyramid was 143.5m. The angle between the face and the horizontal is approximately 53.15¬∞, with different sources providing slightly different exact numbers. If you calculate the length along the centreline of a face of the pyramid, up to the apex, you get 179.45m (from the base length and the vertical height). The ratio of these two lengths is 1:1.25, or 0.8:1. This seems like a good, simple ratio (4 to 5) for the pyramid builders to use, so I used this ratio (approx. 53.13¬∞) when scaling the image data I had.

I used the three tall consecutive courses (courses 71-73) of casing stones as an achor for scaling the image. From visual inspection, the casing stones make up the top 28-30% of the pyramid height, and the three tall courses occur approximately 75% down the casing stones. This puts the three tall courses approx. 39m below the apex, as measured along the angled face, and approx. 31.2m vertically below the apex. The width of the casing stone courses at this point is approx. 46.9m. Note that, the effective angle of the edge of the face itself, when measured in the plane of the face, is 59¬∞.

Construction appears much more consistent near the apex of the pyramid, and below course 55. The construction near to the corners of the pyramid is much more uniform throughout, due to the builders utilising a consistent, repeating pattern in the stonework. Since the top of the pyramid has a higher proportion of its width taken up by these "edge patterns", it appears more uniform.

2. Bulging in the North Face

Vertical Offset of stones from their course Relative height of stones vs their course
Coloured by vertical offset, red = stone is above course average, blue = stone is below course average (measured between centroid of casing stone, and centroid of the whole course) Coloured by relative height to course, red = taller, blue = shorter

At some point in construction, the center of the north-face courses began to bulge upwards, become higher than the edges of the course. There appears to have been a concerted effort to fix this issue, by using shorter blocks for the centers of the course. This effort began below the currently existing courses (below 89), and corrected by roughly 2.7cm per course between courses 75 and 50, and by roughly 1.09cm per course between courses 50 and 10. Above course 10 the issue is resolved.

3. Similarity in stone widths across faces

There is a weak correlation between the average widths of casing stones which are on the same course, but different faces of the pyramid. Mostly, it is noticeable that courses within a face with narrow or wide stones, tend to maintain this pattern around the other faces. Note courses 23, 47 and 52 all have much narrower stones than their adjacent courses.

4. The three large courses

There is a group of three courses which are visually distinctive. They all have very tall stones compared to the average (the largest red band in the above images). These correspond to unified course numbers 71-73, and are an interesting feature of the casing stones. Perhaps the builders encountered a very large seam of high quality limestone during their quarrying, and wished to take advantage of it.

5. Joining or fitting stones

North Face - Width East Face - Width South Face - Width

There appears to be linear patterns of small stones (in dark blue above), which segment the pyramid faces into sections. This may well be evidence of sections of the face being the duty of seperate work crews, and these smaller stones were use to bridge or join the gap between the more average stones laid by the two seperate work crews.

Smaller Stones (North) Possible regions (North)
Coloured by relative width to course, red = wider, blue = narrower Green lines highlight possible regions which were constructed in parallel

Many of these "fitting" stones have angled vertical edges, which would allow them to be maneuvered into place from above. The construction of this angled edge, would likely involve taken one of the normal blocks, and cutting it at an angle into two trapezoids. The smaller trapezoid can then be shaved down to the exact size needed to fit the gap, and dropped into place from above.

6. Chains of offset stones

INSERT SIDE BY SIDE EXAMPLES OF CHAINS OF STONES

There appear to be "chains" of stones, whereby each stone in the chain is offset from the previous by a set amount, and the size of the stones in the chain are all very similar. The number of these occuring mean these aren't random chance, and are somehow related to the building technique.

Some of these chains are offset at an angle similar to the angle of the edge of the face, but some are in the opposite direction.

It is possible, that multiple courses were placed simultaneously, and these chains of stones were the first ones placed amidst otherwise empty courses. Chains of stones in a consistent, overlapping pattern, and with the correct course height, could be useful as an anchor of stability within the otherwise more peicemeal construction.

13. Distribution of stone width by course

Stone width appears either uncorrelated, or only weakly correlated, with course number. However below course 55, the width is much more consistant from course to course. The gaps between stones at the top of the South Face, are due to stones shifting to the left of where they should be. This was possibly caused by an earthquake, or by whoever tumbled the missing top stones down the pyramid.
